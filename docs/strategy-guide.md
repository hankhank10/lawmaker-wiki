# Strategy Guide

The gameplay pages explain *how the systems work*. This guide is about *how to win* — the judgement calls that separate a thriving party from a stalled one. It's the home for strategy, so the individual pages link here rather than repeating it.

## Three core principles

**1. Consistency beats perfection.** The most reliable strategy in the game is to vote consistently with your platform. Voters reward a clear, predictable identity, and coalition partners trust a party that votes the way it said it would. A modest ideology held consistently outperforms a perfect one held erratically — flip-flopping just tells voters you stand for nothing.

**2. Know your voters.** Your country's [electors](gameplay/elections.md#understanding-electors) have real, readable preferences. Study their profiles, watch which issues carry weight, and [commission polls](gameplay/elections.md#polling) before big decisions. Remember recent votes count most, so your record is something you actively manage, not a fixed thing.

**3. Play the long game.** Electoral success takes several cycles — establish a presence, grow your record, then break through. Don't expect instant dominance, and don't treat one bad election as the end.

## Finding your niche

Before you lock in your [pillars](gameplay/parties.md#your-ideology-the-4-pillars), read the political landscape: which ideologies are already represented, where the electors actually cluster, and which voter segment no existing party is serving well. The strongest move is usually to **own an underserved space** rather than compete head-to-head with an established party for the same voters. Whatever position you choose — left, right, libertarian, green, or a sharp single-issue identity — commit to it; even a centrist party has to be *consistently* moderate rather than randomly in the middle.

## Legislation: build the majority before you spend

A [proposal](gameplay/legislation.md) costs 30 PP whether it passes or fails, and a failed vote stays on your record. So the best proposers spend more time negotiating than drafting:

1. Plan the change.
2. [Message](gameplay/communication.md) likely supporters and offer deals (vote trades, coalition backing).
3. Count your votes.
4. Only propose once you have the majority.

**Timing matters too.** Controversial votes early in a cycle fade from voter memory by election day; save popular, campaign-defining proposals for late in the cycle, and avoid divisive ones right before an election. Use **multi-article packages** for genuinely coherent agendas — but remember one unpopular article can sink the whole bill.

## Coalitions: the heart of the game

Because [proportional representation](gameplay/elections.md#the-electoral-system) rarely gives anyone a majority, almost everything runs through coalitions. Build them with parties whose platforms are close to yours, reach out *before* you need their votes, make **specific** offers rather than vague ones, and — above all — honour your commitments. A reputation for reliability is the most valuable asset you can hold at the negotiating table; one broken vote can cost you every future deal.

When distributing [cabinet](gameplay/cabinet.md) posts, keep them roughly proportional to what each partner brings. Greedy splits get voted down.

## Elections: campaign mode

As an election nears, shift gears. Roughly 30 days out, stop taking risks: vote only on safe, popular proposals, poll to track your trajectory, and lean on [campaign events](gameplay/campaigning.md) — stacking Supporter Rallies for a turnout boost, and making sure your party has a [logo](gameplay/parties.md#party-branding) for the branding bonus. Let your polling guide your posture: if you're leading, protect your position; if you're trailing, take a bolder, clearly-differentiated stance.

After the election, win or lose, study the result — which votes helped, which hurt, what the successful parties did — and adjust for next time.

## A few advanced plays

- **The Kingmaker.** A small party that holds the seats to tip a majority can negotiate with both blocs and extract concessions far beyond its size.
- **The Legislative Blitz.** Bank 90–120 PP, then propose several bills in quick succession to dominate the agenda — best right after winning a mandate.
- **The Opposition Block.** Out of government? Coordinate No votes with other excluded parties to deny the government its agenda while you build an alternative coalition.
- **The Trojan Horse.** Bundle a controversial article into a package of popular ones so partners have to swallow it — risky, since they may reject the whole package instead.
- **The Electoral Pivot.** After sustained losses, *gradually* shift your positioning toward where the votes are — slowly enough not to look like flip-flopping. Done clumsily it destroys your brand.

## Managing Political Power

[PP](reference.md#political-power-pp) is your throttle. Keep a ~30–60 PP reserve so you can always act, don't sit at the 120 cap wasting generation, and lean on the free actions — voting, messaging, commenting — which cost nothing and drive most of your influence. In a coalition, take turns proposing so partners share the PP load.

## The bottom line

Strategy in Lawmaker is consistent execution over many cycles, not any single brilliant move. Pick a clear identity, build genuine relationships, manage your record deliberately, and play patiently.
