# Hereditary Monarchy

Some countries have a **hereditary monarchy** alongside their elected institutions. The Crown is a country-level office with its own page and reign history, and — where the [constitution](constitution.md) assigns powers to it — real constitutional authority. The first country to use it is [Avalon](../countries/avalon.md), where the Crown holds the power to grant pardons.

## Claiming the throne

When a throne is **vacant**, active parties can nominate one of their own activists (the nominee must be a current, non-retired activist, and your party can't already be enthroned). Each party gets **one nomination per vacancy**, and nominations can't be withdrawn. You also choose a **dynasty name**; if the nominee wins they take a regnal name like *King Arthur I* or *Queen Eleanor II*.

Each monarchy has its own selection rule — Avalon uses **oldest party** (the longest-established nominating party wins, ties broken by lower party ID). If no one nominates before the deadline, the window extends.

### Accession has a heavy cost

If your nominee wins, your party becomes the reigning Crown and goes **dormant** for the whole reign:

- your party's PP resets to **0** and it loses all its **seats**;
- its **cabinet posts** and **international bloc** memberships are vacated;
- the nominee **leaves the party** to reign as monarch;
- your active identity switches from the party to the Crown.

!!! warning "Don't nominate casually"
    Winning the throne is a prestige move, but it pulls your party out of normal electoral politics for as long as you reign. Pursue it when your party is old enough to have a real chance, you value a prestige role over electoral competition, or the Crown holds powers you want — not when you're electorally strong and active in cabinet or blocs.

## Reigning

While reigning you can post to the social feed as the Crown, and you gain a read-only **Insights** menu to study [polls](elections.md#polling), [elector profiles](elections.md#understanding-electors), and demographics — though you can't commission polls yourself. You can also upload an SVG **coat of arms**.

### Royal assent and veto

In any country with a monarchy, the reigning monarch can pre-register a **veto** on any open proposal. On each proposal the monarch chooses **Allow passage** (the default) or **Veto if it passes**, and can switch between them while voting is open. The choice stays **hidden** — parties vote without knowing it.

At tally time the legislature's vote is counted first. If the bill **fails** on its own, any pending veto is discarded and never revealed. If the bill **passes** and the monarch allowed it, it becomes law. If the bill passes but the monarch vetoed it, it's blocked at royal assent, marked **Vetoed by Monarch**, and a LawBot post announces it. So a veto only ever becomes public when it actually changes the outcome — and monarchs don't vote on legislation, they grant or refuse assent.

### Constitutional crisis

Blocking a bill that the legislature had passed automatically triggers a **Constitutional Crisis** national mood on the country, lasting **90 days** (a further such veto resets the clock). It doesn't shift voter opinions; its effect is surgical and aimed squarely at the monarchy:

- the threshold to pass a **pure abolish-the-monarchy** change is **halved**, and
- the usual constitutional-change **cooldown is bypassed** for such a package.

Add anything else to that package — any other power move, amendment, or repeal — and it loses both benefits. In short, a crisis makes it specifically easier to *remove* the Crown, which is the real cost of using the veto: lean on it too often and the country may assemble a republican majority it couldn't have mustered in calmer weather.

## Leaving the throne

A monarch can **abdicate** at any time: the reign ends, the monarch character retires, and your party is restored — but with **0 seats and 0 PP**, regaining none of what it gave up. Monarchies also have **inactivity** checks; if a reigning monarch goes inactive after warnings, abdication is forced — and a *forced* abdication **disbands** the enthroned party rather than restoring it. Log in regularly while you reign.
